# How Sustainable Fashion Brands Can Win at Keyword Research in 2024

Most fashion brands chase the same keywords — "sustainable clothing," "ethical fashion," "eco-friendly outfits." They compete against H&M's Conscious Collection and ASOS's Responsible Edit, with domain authorities 50x higher than any indie brand. Targeting those keywords is a waste of time.

After auditing keyword strategies for over 30 sustainable fashion brands, I've found that the ones gaining traction aren't winning on volume. They're winning on specificity.

## Why Generic Fashion Keywords Don't Work for Small Brands

The top 10 results for "sustainable fashion" are dominated by Vogue, Elle, and Business of Fashion. A new brand targeting that keyword will never rank on page one — and according to [Ahrefs' analysis of 4 billion pages](https://ahrefs.com/blog/search-traffic-study/), 90.6% of pages get zero organic traffic from Google. The reason is almost always keyword selection.

Small sustainable fashion brands need to target **lower-volume, higher-intent keywords** — the kind a customer types when they're ready to buy, not just browse.

## What I've Seen Work: The Specificity Ladder

When I worked with a London-based sustainable knitwear brand, we shifted their keyword strategy away from "sustainable sweaters" (KD: 62, Volume: 8,100) toward:

- "merino wool sweaters made in England" (KD: 18, Volume: 320)
- "plastic-free knitwear UK" (KD: 9, Volume: 110)
- "GOTS certified wool jumpers" (KD: 7, Volume: 90)

Their organic traffic increased 340% in 4 months. The volume was lower — but the conversion rate was 8x higher because every visitor was already a qualified buyer.

This is the **specificity ladder**: the more specific your keyword, the lower the competition, the higher the intent, and the better it converts.

## Step 1: Start With Your Customer's Language, Not the Industry's

Fashion brands describe their products in brand language. Customers search in their own language. These are almost never the same.

Instead of guessing, use these three sources:

1. **Your own reviews and DMs** — search your Instagram comments and emails for exact phrases customers use to describe your product. "Doesn't pill," "feels like butter," "my toddler can't destroy it" — these become keywords.
2. **Reddit communities** — r/SustainableFashion and r/femalefashionadvice surface unfiltered buyer language. Search "[your product type] recommendation" and read how people describe what they want.
3. **Amazon review mining** — even if you don't sell on Amazon, your competitors do. Their reviews are a free keyword research database.

Filter out anything with KD above 30 unless your domain authority is already above 40.

## Step 3: Map Keywords to Content Types

Every keyword in sustainable fashion maps to one of three content types:

| Keyword type | Example | Best content format |
|---|---|---|
| Product-specific | "GOTS certified merino wool sweater" | Product page or collection page |
| Problem-aware | "how to avoid greenwashing when buying clothes" | Long-form guide |
| Comparison | "Patagonia vs Finisterre fleece" | Comparison article |

Matching the keyword to the right format is what makes Google rank your content. A product-specific keyword written as a blog post will underperform a product page optimized for the same term.

## Step 4: Validate Against What's Actually Ranking

Before writing any content, open an incognito window and search your target keyword. Ask:

- Are the top 3 results brands like mine, or media outlets and aggregators?
- Is the content 800 words or 4,000 words?
- Do results feature images, videos, or just text?
- Is there a featured snippet I can structure my content to capture?

If the top results are all media outlets, it's an informational keyword — you need a guide. If they're product pages, optimize a collection page instead of writing a blog post.

## The One Insight Most Fashion SEO Guides Miss

Seasonality in fashion SEO isn't just about "winter coats" in November. **Search intent shifts within a keyword across the year.**

"Sustainable gifts" in November means "buy something for someone." The same keyword in January means "New Year resolution to shop better." The content that ranks in November won't rank in January because the intent is different — and Google knows it.

Build two versions of seasonal content: one for buying intent (November–December), one for educational intent (January–February). Publish the educational version in January, and update it to buying intent by October.
